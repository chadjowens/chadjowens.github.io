MAKE Brainwaves.
Georgia State / Georgia Tech - Brain Lab

While working on my Masters degree in Computer Information Systems, I became a research assistant for a Computer Science professor. She developed a program called the BrainLab that pulled students from both Georgia Tech and Georgia State. We worked on ground breaking projects that would allow people with severe motor disabilities to interact with their surroundings and control devices using a signal in the brain called the Mu Rhythm.

It was discovered that a person can control their Mu Rhythm - raise or lower it - simply by thinking about an activity. Since it can be controlled (and measured with an EEG machine) the next step was to explore how that could act as a "switch" to allow people with severe motor disabilities a way to browse a custom designed and developed internet browser and email client. We even explored how these "locked-in" patients could navigate and control their surroundings.
‍
During my time as a research assistant, I wrote a short research paper that was published in the SIG CHI (Special Interest Group for Computer Human Interaction) journal and was invited to present the work at a conference.
‍
This job and the research we did was a pivotal reason why I moved into the Experience and Interaction Design field once I completed my degree.

MAKE Ideas.
Chicago Ideas Week - Design Thinking Workshop

While working at FCB in Chicago, I ran a design thinking workshop for Chicago Ideas Week two years in a row.

Chicago Ideas Week is a city-wide program that lets companies open their doors for people get an inside peek. During this event - each time - the sessions had well over 60 people come in to participate in the workshop.

For these sessions, I developed methodology of experience mapping, gamified "playstorming" and hands-on building to have each team come up with fantastic ideas that could solve some very complex issues Chicagoans face daily.

MAKE Games.
Arcade Cabinet - Raspberry Pi

Once a year I get an urge to do a large complicated project. When this urge hits, I grab my kids and we set out to make something that we aren't completely sure we can even accomplish.

For this particular challenge, we built an arcade cabinet from ground up - all completely from scratch.

We downloaded the plans and purchased the wood (and a new table saw). We sourced proper hardware materials and learned how to design, build, paint, assemble, solder, wire and even code a raspberry pi to get a fully functional arcade cabinet set up in our game room.

MAKE Dogs Happy.
Shopping Cart Dog House

Years ago, I was involved in a new business pitch in an attempt to win Digital Agency of Record for Lowe's Home Improvement. The digital agency I was working for decided that it would be a fantastic idea to show Lowe's how passionate we were and how much we wanted to work with them by doing some "extra credit" work. So we set out to do this by holding an internal competition.

The idea was that representatives from each "vertical industry" group within the agency would build a dog house from a Lowe's shopping cart. Then we would present those for fun after the new business pitch during the office tour.

My team (about 4 individuals) worked several late nights making this awesome Winnebago Dog House out of a shopping cart.

This effort definitely let me excercise my "Maker" itch and we won the competition - and the business.

MAKE Run.
Resurrecting a 1986 Honda XR 80.

I've always liked to bring old things to life.

I found an old beat up 1986 Honda XR 80 and although I'm not naturally that mechanical, I was able to totally overhaul it and get it running again.

I also stripped down all the bad paint on it and brought it as close to it's original state as I could with new decals, heat guns and a little elbow grease to remove all the rust.

After watching many you tube videos, I was able to get the engine running fine and enjoy some sunny days riding with my son.

Next step is to replace the clutch. :)
