Goal Based Financial Planning

Once I joined the wealth management team, I worked with business and product owners to define a "native" Citi Financial Planning tool that would allow a customer to set up a comprehensive financial plan without first having to meet with a financial advisor.

This was a major undertaking as the current solution was driven by a financial advisor and they leveraged a complicated 3rd-party tool to create the financial plans.
‍

Journey Mapping
& "Field Research"

The Why & How: One of the first exercises the team jumped into was to define the ideal user journey for a customer at a high level.
‍
This was done in the following ways:
1. Speaking to financial advisors
2. Learning about the consultations and tool used
3. Developing our hypothesis for what the right balance of value vs. complexity is for a customer to start a plan on their own

The Impact: This helped everyone to understand the problem space beyond the brief in a visual way and get a mental model of the experience.
‍
‍

Competitive Audit

The Why & How: As this is a complicated product landscape, there was no shortage of opinions around how the product could work and design treatments people loved. We assembled examples of what the team considered best-in-class solutions for certain aspects of the experience within in the overall journey.

This was done on a Freehand board that was continually added to as the project evolved.

The Impact: This served as a learning for how best-in-class or similar products work, but it also served as a way to manage inputs and feedback from stakeholders.
‍

Defining Need States & Pain Points

The Why & How:
‍Part of our early explorations included a number of workshops where we worked with our product, business and planning partners to better define the customer segments, determine the north star and define needs.
‍
In these workshops we (the full team) revisited wealth specific personas and reflected on how these personas would engage in a digital planning tool, what their pain points may be and "how might we" solve for certain aspects of the experience.

The Impact: This raised many questions during the discussion, but served to better inform the scope of what was to become the MVP effort.

The team followed these workshops with creating a functional requirements document.
‍

Conceptual Explorations
‍
Freehand boards > Sketch >
Back to Freehand

The Why & How: The design team began to concept the experience leveraging Freehand boards and Sketch wireframes to iteratively collaborate on solutions.

This design team workflow was a critical component as our team is spread across US, Canada, Italy and Mexico. This allowed us to have key check-in working sessions and then allow for team members to continue working in their respective time zones.
‍
The Impact: Daily working sessions (and weekly sessions with the larger product and planning team) allowed us to move quickly through questions and blockers and get to a baseline testable prototype.
‍
‍

Baseline Wireframe & User Testing

The Why & How: From our working sessions and baseline hypotheses, we fielded a user test of the full experience to help us get a better understanding of how a customer would engage with a self-directed planning tool.
‍
The Impact: The overall testing was considered successful but there were key learnings that we took back and led us to rethink some aspects of the overall experience. The main one being decoupling the concept of "retirement" from the experience as a required goal.
‍
‍

Revisiting the
Journey

The Why & How: After our first round of user research, we received feedback that led us to question assumptions and rethink parts of the experience. The main shift was to offer a "single goal" set up flow in addition to a more fully comprehensive financial plan.
‍
The Impact: We revisited the overall product positioning and took this opportunity to develop a framework that allowed us to dig deeper into the emotional moments in the journey.
‍
‍

Evolving the Product & Visual Design Treatment

The Why & How: As the project continued to move forward, the MVP experience was pared down from a focus of the larger comprehensive financial plan to one that was more streamlined and focused on setting up a goal that drives consumers toward opening a "robo-investment" product.
‍
The Impact:
During this phase we explored a number of interaction and visual design treatments that varied from conservative to some that pushed the envelope of the current Citi Design System.

Key Activities

Outside of the day-to-day sprint work, I planned and facilitated key stakeholder ideation workshops for the internal Accenture team product stakeholders as well as with potential Accenture clients as part of their new business development efforts. Below are a few examples (from a list of many) used to drive meaningful Design Thinking Workshops.

Target user personas

Early in the process twelve distinct user personas were developed to help guide the ideation and design efforts for the software solution. These personas were based on a combination of stakeholder interviews and workshop sessions.

Functional wireframes and prototypes

Wireframes were created for all features for the entire application. Where needed, clickable prototypes were produced to help inform how the application functioned. Visual designs and pattern library
